Latitude and longitude can be expressed in several different notations, and the
differences matter when you're parsing or generating coordinate data.

The most common distinction is between degrees-minutes-seconds (DMS),
degrees-decimal minutes (DDM), and decimal degrees (DD). DMS looks like
40°26'46"N, 79°58'56"W, splitting each coordinate into whole degrees, minutes
(1/60 of a degree), and seconds (1/60 of a minute). DDM keeps degrees as a
whole number but expresses the remainder as decimal minutes, e.g. 40°26.767'N,
79°58.933'W — this format is common in marine and aviation navigation because
GPS receivers often display it that way. Decimal degrees collapse everything
into a single signed or directional decimal number, e.g. 40.446188, -79.982025,
which is what most APIs, databases, and programming libraries expect since it's
just two floats.

On sign conventions: decimal degrees can use a leading negative sign for south
or west (the convention ISO 6709 and most software use), or they can use a
trailing hemisphere letter (N/S/E/W) instead of a sign, which is more common in
DMS/DDM contexts and in older or more human-facing formats. Latitude ranges -90
to 90, longitude -180 to 180 (or sometimes 0-360 for longitude in some
astronomical/oceanographic contexts).

A few other notations worth knowing about:

ISO 6709 is a formal string standard for representing geographic coordinates,
e.g. +40.446195-079.982195/, including optional altitude and a coordinate
reference system identifier at the end.

UTM (Universal Transverse Mercator) doesn't use lat/long at all — it divides
the Earth into 60 zones and gives a position as zone number + easting +
northing in meters, e.g. 17T 589948 4477138. It's popular in surveying and
military contexts because distances within a zone are easy to compute in plain
meters.

MGRS (Military Grid Reference System) builds on UTM but adds a grid-zone
designator and uses concatenated alphanumeric strings, e.g. 17T PE 89948 77138,
common in military and some emergency-response applications.

Geohash encodes lat/long into a single short alphanumeric string (like
9q8yyk8ytpxr) by interleaving bits of latitude and longitude, useful for
spatial indexing and proximity search in databases.

Plus codes (Google's Open Location Code) are similar in spirit, producing
strings like 849VCWC8+R9, designed to be usable even without street addresses.

One more thing that trips people up: the datum. The same nominal
"latitude/longitude" numbers can refer to slightly different physical locations
depending on the reference ellipsoid/datum used. WGS84 is the de facto global
standard now (used by GPS), but older datums like NAD27 or local national
datums can differ by tens to hundreds of meters for the same coordinate pair,
so when precision matters it's worth confirming which datum a given dataset
uses.
